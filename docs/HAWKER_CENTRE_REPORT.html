1. Hawkers Are Unlike Any Other Category

Hawker centres are government-allocated infrastructure, not market-driven businesses. This makes their spatial DNA fundamentally different from restaurants, cafes, or retail.

The signature: high transit dependency, near-zero competitive pressure. Hawker centres are placed where people already are (HDB blocks, MRT stations), not where market forces drive them.

The Tourist/CBD cluster (714 stalls) is distinctive: 7x the tourist draw (1,240 vs ~50-180), 5x the MRT access (0.69 stations/hex), lower residential density. These are Maxwell, Chinatown Complex, Lau Pa Sat, and the Bugis/Kampong Glam food courts. They serve a fundamentally different population than the suburban heartland hawkers.

The Dense HDB cluster (684 stalls) has the highest residential density (35,704/km²) but the lowest tourist draw (36). These are the neighbourhood hawker centres built as part of HDB estates — 100% resident-serving.

4. The Two Worlds of Singapore Hawkers

CBD/Tourist hawkers vs HDB hawkers: cosine similarity = 0.031

Essentially zero. In the 37-dimensional context space, a Chinatown hawker stall and a Bedok hawker stall are as different as two random points. Same food, same price, completely different urban role:

CBD hawkers are embedded in nightlife/tourism context (nearest non-hawker neighbors are wine bars and bistros). HDB hawkers are embedded in neighbourhood retail (nearest neighbors are cafes, bakeries, and convenience stores). The same institution serves completely different communities.
